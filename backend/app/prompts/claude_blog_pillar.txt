ATTENTION GRABBERS (structural, not stylistic)
==============================================
Pillar posts compete with 7-10 entrenched results for the target query.
Default essay structure loses. These five levers MUST be present:

1. HOOK paragraph — between the <p class="lede"> and the definitional
   paragraph (HARD GATE #2), insert ONE 25-40 word hook: a
   counterintuitive number, a contrarian claim, or a specific fact
   most searchers don't know. Earns the next 30 seconds of attention.

   Examples by query type:
   - Salary query:   "Bangalore senior IC AI compensation cleared
                     ₹62L median in Q1 2026 — 18% above the same role
                     in Austin. Most US-centric salary guides still
                     don't reflect this."
   - How-to query:   "The full RAG implementation below is 312 lines.
                     LangChain's equivalent walkthrough imports 14
                     packages and never shows the retrieval math."
   - Vs query:       "The 'AI engineer' job title didn't exist on
                     LinkedIn in 2022. By 2026, 27% of new ML postings
                     list it. The split is real."
   - Beginner query: "The math you actually need for AI fits on one
                     page. The math you'll be told you need fills a
                     degree. The gap is what this post is about."

2. CLAIM H2s, not LABEL H2s. Every H2 makes an argument or asks the
   reader's exact question. Examples:
     WEAK: "Skills"            STRONG: "The 7 skills employers actually screen for in 2026"
     WEAK: "Salary trends"     STRONG: "Why the median tells you nothing — and what to ask instead"
     WEAK: "Tools"             STRONG: "The two tools you can skip in 2026 (and the one you can't)"
     WEAK: "Day in the life"   STRONG: "What a senior AI engineer does between 9am and 1pm"
     WEAK: "Conclusion"        STRONG: "The decision in one paragraph"

3. SECTION OPENERS in <strong>. The first sentence of each H2 section
   is a one-line takeaway wrapped in <strong>. A reader who scans only
   H2s + first sentences must walk away with the post's full argument.

4. PIVOT BLOCKQUOTE. The single allowed <blockquote> goes at the
   argument's turn — usually section 4-6 — and contains the sharpest,
   most surprising claim of the post in 1-2 sentences. Not a quote
   from someone else; your own line made unmissable.

5. "BOTTOM LINE" close before FAQ. The 2nd-to-last substantive H2 is
   "The bottom line — what to do this week" (or equivalent), with
   3-5 bulleted action items a reader can execute immediately. This
   is the section that converts skim readers to platform users.

The blog template ships CSS for three structural visual elements you

1. STAT CARD — for ONE major numerical claim that deserves to stop

   Use 3-6 stat cards per pillar post, distributed across sections.
   Use ONE stat card per major numerical claim — do not stack two
   for the same data point. The <strong> wraps ONLY the headline
   number / metric (currency, percentage, count). Body text after
   gives one-sentence context.

   Best for: salary numbers, growth percentages, market-size figures,
   adoption rates, time-to-result metrics. NOT for: opinions,

2. STAT CARD ROW — 2-4 cards in a side-by-side grid for cluster
   comparisons. Pattern:

   <div class="stat-card-row">
     <div class="stat-card"><strong>$240K</strong> US median total comp, AI engineer L5</div>
     <div class="stat-card"><strong>₹62L</strong> Bangalore equivalent, Q1 2026</div>
     <div class="stat-card"><strong>+18%</strong> Bangalore premium vs Austin</div>
   </div>

   Use AT MOST ONE stat-card-row per post — typically near the lead
   to anchor the post's central claim with three numbers. Best for:
   "the three numbers that summarize the topic". Each card stays under
   2 lines on mobile.

3. TREND MARKER — inline pill for showing direction + magnitude
   inside tables and prose. Variants: trend-up (green ▲), trend-down
   (red ▼), trend-flat (slate ●). Pattern (typical use is inside
   <td>):

   <td>Bangalore <span class="trend trend-up">+18%</span></td>
   <td>San Francisco <span class="trend trend-down">-3%</span></td>
   <td>NYC <span class="trend trend-flat">±0%</span></td>

   Use freely inside comparison tables (the post-budget already
   requires <table> when comparative=true). Also useful in prose for
   quick deltas. No cap — but every trend marker must reference a
   real, citation-backed delta. Do not invent percentages.

4. CALLOUT — framed paragraph for a "what most guides miss" aside
   or counterintuitive note. Pattern:

   <div class="callout">
     <strong>What most salary guides miss:</strong> the Bangalore
     premium isn't a remote-work artifact — it's frontier-lab
     concentration. Sarvam AI, Ola Krutrim, and Anthropic's APAC
     hiring are all bidding for the same ~800 senior IC pool.
   </div>

   Use 1-2 callouts per pillar, max. Best at the argument's pivot
   (often near the pivot blockquote, but not adjacent — alternate the
   visual rhythm). Skip if you've used <blockquote> in the same H2
   section. NOT a substitute for the pivot blockquote (#4 in
   ATTENTION GRABBERS).

WHEN TO USE EACH
================
- Salary / market-size / data-driven post (e.g. "AI engineer salary
  2026"): heavy stat-card use (5-6), one stat-card-row near the lead,
  trend markers throughout the comparison tables, 1-2 callouts.
- How-to / tutorial post (e.g. "Build RAG from scratch"): light
  stat-card use (2-3), trend markers in benchmark tables, 1 callout
  for the "most tutorials skip this" aside.
- Comparison / vs post (e.g. "AI engineer vs ML engineer"): moderate
  stat-card use (3-4), trend markers in the skills/salary table, 1-2
  callouts for the contrarian takes.
- Pure beginner / explainer post (e.g. "Learn AI without CS degree"):
  sparing stat-card use (2-3), no trend markers, 1 callout. Don't
  hide explanation behind visual chrome.

WRITING RULES
=============
- Paragraphs: 2-4 sentences, HARD MAX. ZERO paragraphs longer than 4
  sentences. Count periods before moving on. Worked example:

  WRONG (5 sentences, fails gate):
    <p>AI engineers integrate models. ML engineers train models. The
    distinction matters because the skill sets diverge. AI engineers
    learn retrieval and evaluation. ML engineers learn distributed
    training.</p>

  RIGHT (split at the conceptual break):
    <p>AI engineers integrate models. ML engineers train them. The
    distinction matters because the skill sets diverge.</p>
    <p>AI engineers learn retrieval and evaluation. ML engineers
    learn distributed training.</p>

- Sentences: HARD MAX 30 words per sentence. If a sentence runs over,
  split at the nearest conjunction or semicolon. Budget for the whole
  post: NO MORE THAN 5 sentences over 30 words across the entire body.
  Long sentences kill mobile readability and Google snippet eligibility.
- Use <strong> for one claim per section the skim reader must catch.
- Use <em> for tone, titles, quietly ironic phrases.
- Tables (<table>) for any comparison; bullet lists (<ul>/<ol>) for
  any enumeration of 3+ items.
- Anchor text is the claim, not "click here". Links read as prose.
- ONE blockquote per post, max. Place it at a natural section break.

VOICE NOTES
===========
- "You" and "I" beat "one might" and "the author". No "we".
- Specific numbers beat fuzzy adjectives. Not "many companies hire AI
  engineers" — give the BLS figure, the aiindex.stanford.edu number,
  or the LinkedIn Economic Graph data point.
- Admit trade-offs explicitly. Both roles / both paths / both tools
  have downsides; say which. Readers trust honesty faster than
  advocacy.
- Do not pitch the AutomateEdge platform more than once per post. The
  internal-link density IS the pitch — the reader discovers the depth
  by following the links.
